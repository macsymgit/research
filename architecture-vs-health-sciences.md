## 2. Co-op Rates — The Real Numbers Side by Side

> Co-op employment rate is one of the most important real-world signals for a program's job market health.

| Term | Architecture | Architectural Engineering | Health Sciences |
|------|-------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| Fall 2024 | **>98%** | ~96.8% (UW-wide) | ~95%+ |
| Spring 2024 | >98% | ~93.2% | 90s% |
| **Spring 2025** | **>98%** | ~75–77% (UW-wide) | **71% ← LOWEST** |
| COVID Spring 2020 | ~90%+ | ~86.5% (UW-wide) | 86.5% (prior all-time low) |
| Typical "good year" | **>98%** | 93–97% | 90–95% |

**What this tells you:**

- **Architecture co-op is uniquely resilient.** Even during COVID and the 2025 economic downturn, Architecture maintained near-100% placement because architecture firms hire specifically for studio work that can't be easily paused.

- **Engineering (AE) tracks the university average** — strong in fall, weaker in spring. Spring 2025 dipped due to macro conditions but engineering faculties outperformed health.

- **Health Sciences is the most vulnerable in downturns.** Its placements depend heavily on public sector (hospitals, health agencies) that freeze hiring under budget pressure. Spring 2025's 71% rate — historically unprecedented, worse than COVID — is a serious warning signal. The average masks the risk.

### Critical Nuance for Health Sciences

**The health sector is in crisis-level shortage — but a BSc Health Sciences does NOT directly fill it.**

The vacancies are for:
- Registered Nurses (require nursing degree, not health sciences BSc)
- Physicians (require MD, ~12–14 years from HS)
- Occupational Therapists (require MOT, ~6–7 years from HS)
- Personal Support Workers (no degree required; low wages)

A BSc Health Sciences graduate competes for research coordinator, health promotion, and policy analyst roles — where the shortage is far more moderate. **You need further credentials to access the high-demand, well-paying parts of the health market.**

## 7. AI Disruption Risk

| Factor | Architecture | Architectural Engineering | Health Sciences |
|--------|-------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| **Tasks at AI risk** | Drafting, rendering, energy sim, early-phase layouts | Clash detection, compliance checking, energy modeling runs | Admin work, preliminary diagnosis, data entry |
| **Tasks resilient to AI** | Design judgment, client relationships, creative vision, site context | Licensed engineer sign-off (legally required), complex structural judgment, site inspection | Clinical judgment, patient care, public health fieldwork, policy leadership |
| **Regulatory protection** | Moderate — AI can't be the OAA-licensed Architect | **Strong** — only P.Eng. can seal engineering drawings; AI cannot get a license | Moderate — clinical roles require professional registration; analyst roles less protected |
| **Junior role risk** | **High** — production/drafting jobs most threatened | Medium — junior engineers shift to AI oversight vs. doing | Medium — research assistant and coordinator roles face automation |
| **5-year risk level** | ⚠️ Medium-High | ✅ Low-Medium | ⚠️ Medium |

**Key insight:** Architectural Engineering has the strongest structural protection against AI displacement because **Canadian law requires a licensed P.Eng. to seal engineering designs** — AI cannot obtain that seal. Health Sciences clinical careers have similar regulatory protection (MD, RN, OT), but the BSc-only analyst roles have less.

## 11. Bottom Line Recommendation

### The Honest Verdict

**If your primary goal is career stability + financial return + clear path:**
> **Choose Architectural Engineering.**
> It wins on co-op reliability, starting salary, financial efficiency, AI resilience, and path clarity. You graduate in 5 years into a structural shortage of licensed engineers. Co-op earnings nearly offset tuition. P.Eng. licensure is faster and less expensive than OAA. The job market is supported by Canada's $100B+ infrastructure investment and housing construction boom.

**If your primary goal is creative leadership + prestige + international career:**
> **Choose Architecture.**
> Waterloo Architecture is top-5 globally and the best in Canada. Co-op placement is near-100% even in downturns. You'll work at internationally renowned firms, spend a semester in Rome, and have one of the most recognized design credentials in North America. Accept the trade-off: longer, more expensive path; lower early salary; harder licensure.

**If your primary goal is healthcare and you have a specific professional school plan:**
> **Health Sciences can work — but only if you know where you're going.**
> If you are committed to medicine, pharmacy, OT, or PT — and have the grades and motivation to follow through — the Health Sciences BSc is a solid pre-professional foundation. Waterloo's co-op adds clinical experience most pre-med students don't have. **But if you don't have a clear next step, the BSc alone delivers the weakest co-op pay, the most volatile placement rates, and the lowest direct-employment salary of the three options.** In that case, Architecture or AE is a safer choice.

### One-Line Summary for Each Person

| You are... | Go with |
|-----------|---------|
| Strong in STEM, want good salary, want to build things | **Architectural Engineering** |
| Creative, have a design portfolio, OK with a long path | **Architecture** |
| Committed to medicine/clinical career, have a plan | **Health Sciences** |
| Unsure about healthcare, just interested in "health" | **Architecture or AE instead** |
| Want the highest possible long-term income, OK with 12+ years | **Health Sciences → Medicine** |
| Want the safest, most financially efficient path | **Architectural Engineering** |
