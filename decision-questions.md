# Self-Reflection Questions: Architecture vs. Architectural Engineering vs. 3D Animation vs. Health Sciences

> Use this document as a personal interview. Answer each question honestly and in writing — vague mental answers will mislead you. This is not a quiz; there are no wrong answers. The goal is to surface what you actually value, not what sounds good.

---

## Part 1: What Kind of Work Excites You?

These questions probe your core interests before any career strategy is applied.

---

### Q1. When you build or make something, what part feels most satisfying?

**Prompt:** Think about projects — school assignments, hobbies, anything you've created.

- Was the best part *the concept and vision* — deciding what it should be, how it should feel, what story it tells? → **Architecture signal**
- Was the best part *solving a technical constraint* — making it structurally work, optimizing it, debugging it? → **Architectural Engineering signal**
- Was the best part *making it look real and move beautifully* — the visual quality, the animation, the polished render? → **3D Animation signal**
- Was the best part *understanding how it affects people* — the human outcome, the health or behavioral result? → **Health Sciences signal**

Write 2–3 sentences describing a specific project and what part you enjoyed most.

---

### Q2. Imagine your ideal workday 10 years from now. What are you doing?

**Prompt:** Be specific — not a job title, but the actual activities hour by hour.

- Presenting a design concept to a client, sketching ideas, reviewing materials for a building you're authoring → **Architecture**
- Running structural calculations, reviewing engineering drawings, coordinating MEP systems on a building → **Architectural Engineering**
- Animating a character for a film shot, lighting a 3D scene, reviewing VFX in dailies with a director → **3D Animation**
- Analyzing health data, working in a clinic, designing a public health program, conducting research → **Health Sciences**
- I genuinely cannot imagine any of these clearly → **This is important information. You may be choosing too early.**

---

### Q3. What would you do for free if money didn't matter?

**Prompt:** This question cuts through pragmatic reasoning.

- Sketch buildings and cities, visit architecture, design spaces → **Architecture**
- Learn how structures work, experiment with building systems, model energy flows → **Architectural Engineering**
- Create 3D art, animate characters, make short films, build virtual worlds → **3D Animation**
- Volunteer in health care settings, read about medicine or disease, work on community wellness → **Health Sciences**
- None of the above → **None of these may be the right fit. Investigate further.**

---

### Q4. When you read or watch something that grips you, what domain is it in?

**Prompt:** What topics do you seek out unprompted?

Examples to consider:
- Do you watch Dezeen, visit architecture exhibitions, read about famous buildings? → **Architecture**
- Do you find yourself curious about how skyscrapers don't fall, how HVAC works, what makes buildings energy-efficient? → **Architectural Engineering**
- Do you watch VFX breakdowns, follow animation studios on social media, study how CGI in films is made? → **3D Animation**
- Do you follow healthcare news, read about epidemics, care about the healthcare system or public health crises? → **Health Sciences**

## Part 2: Self-Knowledge — Strengths and Style

---

### Q5. Are you stronger in math/science, art/design, or digital creation?

**Prompt:** Be honest. All four programs require different skill mixes.

| If you are... | Consider |
|---------------|---------|
| Strong in STEM, weak in visual design | **Architectural Engineering** is more aligned; Architecture and 3D Animation require portfolio work |
| Strong in both STEM and visual/creative work | **Architecture or AE** — your bottleneck is passion, not aptitude |
| Strong in digital art and technical tools, moderate in math | **3D Animation** — requires artistic skill + technical pipeline knowledge, not heavy calculus |
| Strong in science/biology, weaker in structural math | **Health Sciences** is a better fit; AE's math load is engineering-level |
| Creative but not strong in math | **Architecture** (moderate math) or **3D Animation** (minimal math) over AE (heavy engineering math) |

Rate yourself 1–10 on each axis honestly: Math/Engineering __ / Visual-Creative __ / Digital-Technical __.

---

### Q6. Do you have a portfolio or creative work to show?

**Prompt:** Architecture and 3D Animation both require portfolios for admission. AE does not.

- I have sketchbooks, design projects, photography, models, graphic work, or architectural drawings → **Architecture is accessible to you**
- I have 3D renders, animations, digital art, game mods, short films, or VFX work → **3D Animation is accessible to you**
- I have both physical design work and digital creative work → **Both Architecture and 3D Animation are options**
- I have strong STEM performance but no meaningful portfolio → **AE is the most practical path; Architecture and 3D Animation admission will be harder**
- I have neither a portfolio nor strong STEM grades — what do I actually have? → **Revisit this before applying anywhere**

Write down 3 pieces of creative or technical work you could put in front of an admissions committee right now.

---

### Q7. How do you handle ambiguity and open-ended problems?

**Prompt:** The programs train you very differently.

- I thrive with problems that have many valid answers and I enjoy defending my interpretation → **Architecture** (design crits require this)
- I prefer problems that have a correct answer I can solve through calculation and analysis → **Architectural Engineering**
- I enjoy iterating on visual work until it "feels right" — I trust my eye and refine through feedback → **3D Animation** (production dailies and director notes require this)
- I prefer working with people and data where solutions are measurable in health outcomes → **Health Sciences**

Think of a recent open-ended project. Did you feel energized or frustrated by the lack of clear answers?

---

### Q8. How important is being the creative decision-maker vs. the technical expert?

**Prompt:** In practice, these roles interact but are distinct.

- I want to be the person who *decides* what the building looks like, how it feels, what it means → **Architecture**
- I want to be the person who *ensures* the building works — structurally, mechanically, sustainably → **Architectural Engineering**
- I want to be the person who *brings digital worlds and characters to life* visually → **3D Animation**
- I want to be the person who *improves people's health* directly or through systems → **Health Sciences**

The question isn't which role is more important — it's which one you'd rather inhabit.

## Part 3: Career and Financial Realities

---

### Q9. How much does your salary in your late 20s matter to you?

**Prompt:** Be concrete. What standard of living do you need at age 28–30?

| If you want/need by age 28–30... | Best path |
|----------------------------------|-----------|
| $62,000–$75,000+ (first job out of school) | **Architectural Engineering** (BASc, 5 years) |
| $50,000–$70,000 is fine, career trajectory matters more | **Architecture** (BAS+MArch, 6–7 years) |
| $44,000–$60,000 and I'll build from there through my portfolio | **3D Animation** (4 years, but volatile job market) |
| I'm OK with $45,000–$55,000 at 28 if I'm heading to grad school | **Health Sciences** (BSc direct employment only) |
| I want $200,000+ eventually and I'll accept 12–14 hard years | **Health Sciences → Medicine** |

Write the number: "I need at least $_______ per year by age ___."

---

### Q10. How do you feel about carrying student debt during your 20s?

**Prompt:** The programs have significantly different net costs.

- **Architecture (BAS+MArch)**: Net ~$55,000–$85,000 out-of-pocket after co-op earnings. MArch alone costs ~$40,000 with no co-op.
- **Architectural Engineering (BASc)**: Net ~$0–$20,000 after co-op earnings. Co-op often covers most tuition.
- **3D Animation (Sheridan)**: Net ~$30,000–$42,000. Lower total cost (~$40–50K) but only ~$10K in internship earnings to offset it.
- **Health Sciences (BSc only)**: Net ~$0–$15,000 (cheap degree) — but a graduate degree for clinical work adds $20,000–$120,000+.

Questions to ask yourself:
- Do you have family support, or will you need to be fully self-funding?
- Does $50,000+ in debt for a degree you're not certain about feel manageable?
- If you go Health Sciences and discover at Year 3 you don't want med school, what do you do with a BSc alone?
- If you go 3D Animation and the job market doesn't recover, can you handle freelance instability with student debt?

---

### Q11. Are you prepared for the full timeline to professional standing?

**Prompt:** All four paths have different lengths. Reality-check yourself.

| Path | Years from High School to Full Professional Standing |
|------|------------------------------------------------------|
| Health Sciences → MD | 12–14 years |
| Architecture → OAA Architect | 9–11 years |
| Architectural Engineering → P.Eng. | 8–9 years |
| Health Sciences → OT/PT | 6–7 years |
| Health Sciences BSc (direct) | 4–5 years → but limited salary ceiling |
| **3D Animation → workforce** | **~4 years → but no credential, no licensure, portfolio-dependent** |

Ask yourself: Can you sustain motivation and financial stability for that full duration? Have you sustained a difficult commitment for 3+ years before? What evidence do you have?

Note: 3D Animation gets you to the workforce fastest, but with the least professional protection. Speed to market ≠ speed to stability.

---

### Q12. What's your backup plan if your first-choice path doesn't work out?

**Prompt:** Every program has failure modes. Test your resilience.

- **Architecture**: What if you don't get into MArch? (BAS alone has limited career ceiling.) What if design firms aren't hiring when you graduate?
- **Architectural Engineering**: What if you discover in Year 2 that you hate structural calculations? AE is harder to pivot out of mid-degree.
- **3D Animation**: What if the animation/VFX industry is still contracting when you graduate in 2030? What if AI has automated the entry-level roles you trained for? What's your plan B with a 3D animation degree?
- **Health Sciences**: What if your GPA isn't competitive for med school by Year 3? A BSc Health Sciences alone is a weak standalone credential for high-paying jobs.

Write your contingency plan for each scenario.

## Part 4: The Health Sciences Test

*Answer these only if Health Sciences is genuinely on your list.*

---

### Q13. Why Health Sciences specifically — and what comes after the BSc?

**Prompt:** This is the most important question for anyone considering Health Sciences.

The BSc Health Sciences does NOT qualify you for the high-paying, high-demand health jobs. It is a pre-professional foundation. The degree earns $45,000–$65,000 alone. The valuable jobs require:

- **Medicine (MD)**: 4 more years of school + 2–5 years residency. Competitive MCAT, GPA, CASPer required.
- **Occupational Therapy / Physiotherapy**: 2 more years of grad school (MOT/MPT). Competitive.
- **Public Health / Epidemiology**: MSc or PhD. Moderate competition.
- **Pharmacy (PharmD)**: 4 years pharmacy school. Competitive.

Answer directly: What is your specific next step after the BSc, and what is your evidence that you can reach it?

If you don't have a clear answer, **Health Sciences is likely not the right path for you right now.**

---

### Q14. Are you drawn to health care because you want to help people, or because of the job security/salary?

**Prompt:** This matters because the paths are completely different.

- If you want to *directly care for patients* — Medicine, Nursing, OT, PT are the right tracks. Health Sciences is a valid pre-professional path.
- If you want *job stability and good salary* in health — Architectural Engineering actually has better co-op employment rates, higher starting salary, and more reliable career trajectory than a Health Sciences BSc alone.
- If you're passionate about *public health systems and policy* — Health Sciences makes sense, but know the salary ceiling is $65–$80K without a graduate degree.

What specifically about health attracts you? Write 3 sentences without mentioning salary.

---

### Q15. How do you feel about Health Sciences' co-op vulnerability?

**Prompt:** Spring 2025's co-op employment rate for Health Sciences hit 71% — the lowest ever recorded at Waterloo, worse than COVID.

- Architecture maintained >98% even during COVID and Spring 2025.
- Architectural Engineering tracked the university average (~75–77% in Spring 2025).
- Health Sciences is disproportionately dependent on public sector (hospitals, health agencies) that freeze co-op hiring under budget pressure.

Ask yourself: If you graduated during a public health funding freeze and couldn't find a co-op placement, what would you do? Can your finances and mental health handle that uncertainty?

## Part 5: The 3D Animation Test

*Answer these only if 3D Animation is genuinely on your list.*

---

### Q16. Why 3D Animation — passion or perception?

**Prompt:** The animation industry looks glamorous from the outside. Test your motivation.

- Do you *actively create* 3D art, animations, or digital content in your spare time? → **Strong signal — you have intrinsic motivation**
- Do you mostly *watch and appreciate* animated films, VFX, and games but haven't created much yourself? → **Weaker signal — you may be a fan, not a practitioner**
- Are you drawn to animation because it seems creative and fun compared to "boring" alternatives? → **Red flag — every career has tedious parts; animation's tedious parts involve hours of technical iteration**

The best animators are people who animate *because they can't stop themselves*, not because it seems cool. Which describes you?

---

### Q17. Are you comfortable with the job market reality?

**Prompt:** Canada Job Bank rates 3D animation outlook in Ontario as **"Very Limited"** — the lowest possible rating. This is driven by:

- Mass layoffs at Canadian studios (2023–2026)
- AI automation displacing production roles
- Post-pandemic production contraction
- Substantial pool of experienced unemployed artists competing for the same jobs you'll want

Compare this to:
- Architecture and Architectural Engineering: both rated **"Moderate"** (balanced supply/demand)

Ask yourself honestly: Are you prepared to enter one of the most competitive creative job markets in Canada? Do you have a strategy beyond "I'll be good enough"?

---

### Q18. How do you feel about having NO professional licensing or credential protection?

**Prompt:** This is the single biggest structural difference between 3D Animation and the other programs.

- **P.Eng.** (Architectural Engineering): AI cannot legally replace you — a human P.Eng. must seal engineering drawings. This creates a regulatory floor for demand.
- **OAA Architect** (Architecture): A human architect must stamp architectural drawings. Moderate protection.
- **3D Animator**: No licensing. No regulatory requirement. If an AI can do your job, a studio can replace you. There is zero legal barrier.

This doesn't mean animators will disappear — but it means the industry can contract without hitting a regulatory wall. How does that make you feel about a 40-year career?

---

### Q19. Can you handle project-based employment and income instability?

**Prompt:** The animation/VFX industry works differently from architecture or engineering.

- **34% of animation workers are self-employed** (highest of all four programs)
- Many animators work on **6–18 month contracts** with gaps between projects
- **Layoffs are sudden** — studios routinely cut 10–50% of staff when a project wraps
- Benefits coverage is lower (85.8% vs. 97.7% in engineering)
- Crunch periods mean 60–80+ hour weeks before deadlines

Compare this to:
- Architecture: 24% self-employed, 93% full-time, project-based but with more permanent positions
- Architectural Engineering: ~10% self-employed, ~96% full-time, mostly permanent employment

Can your financial situation and mental health handle months without income between contracts? Write honestly.

---

### Q20. Is this program proven, or are you betting on a brand?

**Prompt:** The Sheridan 3DAET program is **brand new** — first cohort September 2026. It has:

- ✅ Sheridan's legendary animation reputation (#1 Canada, #2 globally)
- ✅ SIRT partnership with industry-grade facilities
- ✅ 50+ years of animation alumni network
- ❌ Zero graduates from this specific program
- ❌ No employment data for this degree
- ❌ No proven track record

Waterloo Architecture and Architectural Engineering have decades of graduate outcomes data. You're comparing proven programs to a promise.

How much weight are you putting on the Sheridan brand vs. the specific program's track record? Is that a reasonable bet?

## Part 6: The Architecture vs. Architectural Engineering Test

*Answer these if you're deciding between the two building-focused programs.*

---

### Q21. When you look at a building, what do you notice first?

**Prompt:** Your instinctive attention reveals your natural orientation.

- How it looks, how it makes you feel, the proportions, the materials, the story it tells → **Architecture**
- How it stands up, how the structure works, whether it's efficiently designed, what the mechanical systems are → **Architectural Engineering**
- Both equally, with genuine curiosity → Either could work — go deeper with Q22.

---

### Q22. Would you rather lead the design or enable the design?

**Prompt:** In practice:
- **Architects** author the building. They decide what it should be. They defend their vision. They bear responsibility for the design outcome.
- **Architectural Engineers** make the architect's vision technically real. They solve the engineering problems. They ensure what's designed can actually be built and will perform.

Neither is subordinate — they're genuinely collaborative and interdependent. But you will spend 40 years in one role. Which feels more like you?

---

### Q23. How do you feel about the Architecture burnout data?

**Prompt:** Waterloo Architecture reports that >95% of students experience burnout. Studio culture involves late nights, harsh critiques, intense peer competition, and the psychological weight of defending your creative vision publicly.

- I've thrived in high-pressure creative environments before; I know I can handle this → **Architecture may suit you**
- I need predictable hours, clear criteria for success, and technical rather than subjective evaluation → **Architectural Engineering is a better cultural fit**
- I'm unsure — I've never been tested this way → **Visit the Cambridge campus; talk to current Architecture students**

Write about a time you worked under severe deadline/performance pressure. How did you respond?

---

### Q24. How important is international co-op placement to you?

**Prompt:**
- Architecture students regularly get co-op placements at globally recognized firms in New York, London, Tokyo, Singapore (KPF, Arquitectonica, etc.).
- Architectural Engineering co-op is strong but primarily Ontario and Canada-based.

If spending a work term in Tokyo or London matters to you, Architecture's co-op network is a genuine differentiator.

---

### Q25. Can you build a strong visual portfolio before applications?

**Prompt:** Architecture requires a portfolio submission. AE does not.

A competitive Waterloo Architecture portfolio shows:
- Design thinking (sketches, concept development, not just finished drawings)
- Spatial awareness (models, floor plans, 3D work)
- Artistic ability (drawing, photography, mixed media)
- A personal voice

Do you have 6–10 pieces of work that show this? If not, do you have 12 months to build it?

If you genuinely have nothing and can't build it quickly, AE is the more realistic admission target.

## Part 7: Values and Identity

---

### Q26. Which professional title or identity do you want to hold, and why?

**Prompt:** "Architect," "Professional Engineer," "Animator," and "Physician" carry different social weight and open different doors.

- **Architect (OAA)**: High cultural prestige; recognized globally; associated with creative authorship; 24% self-employed; enables independent practice.
- **P.Eng.**: Strong professional prestige in technical fields; legally required to seal engineering drawings; respected in construction and industry; ~10% self-employed.
- **3D Animator / VFX Artist**: No protected title; prestige comes from your credits (films, studios, awards) not credentials; 34% self-employed; global creative community.
- **MD / OT / PT / MPH**: Healthcare professional titles; high social trust; patient-facing or research-facing; regulated and recession-resistant at the clinical level.

Which of these identities — if you held it at 40 — would make you feel you'd achieved what you set out to do? Write your answer.

---

### Q27. How important is creative recognition and public impact to you?

**Prompt:** The four programs have different relationships to public visibility.

- Buildings designed by architects become part of cities. People interact with them for centuries. Design authorship is visible.
- Engineering work is essential but often invisible — you know the bridge stays up because of the engineer, but the architect gets named.
- Animation/VFX work is seen by millions — your name is in the credits, your art is on screen — but few people know who animated which shot. The director gets the credit.
- Health careers create direct human impact — you see the outcome in the person you treated.

Which form of impact matters most to you: *visible design legacy, technical excellence, art seen by millions, or direct human care*?

---

### Q28. How do you feel about prestige and social recognition?

**Prompt:** Be honest — prestige matters to some people and that's valid.

| Program | Prestige Profile |
|---------|-----------------|
| Waterloo Architecture | Top 5 globally; name recognition in design circles worldwide; alumni at starchitect firms |
| Waterloo Architectural Engineering | Not independently ranked; respected within construction/engineering industry |
| Sheridan 3D Animation | #1 Canada, #2 globally for animation; "Harvard of Animation"; alumni at Disney, Pixar — enormous prestige within the animation world |
| Waterloo Health Sciences → Medicine | Physician is among the highest-prestige professions in Canadian society |

If external recognition from peers and family influences your decision, it's better to know that now than to pretend it doesn't.

---

### Q29. What does "a good life" look like to you at 40?

**Prompt:** This is the real question underneath all the others.

Write 10 sentences describing your life at 40 without mentioning your job title. Then ask: which program's career path produces that life?

Consider:
- Where are you living?
- What does your day look like?
- How do you feel about your work on a Tuesday afternoon?
- What are you proud of?
- How much do financial pressures affect you?
- How many hours a week are you working?
- How stable is your employment?

Then match that picture to the data in these documents.

**Scoring guide:**

### Q31. What would you regret more: choosing wrong, or not trying?

**Prompt:** Four different failure modes.

- **Architecture risk**: You commit 7 years + significant debt and discover at Year 4 that you don't love design enough to do it for 40 years. Switching is costly.
- **AE risk**: You choose the financially safe path, but you spend your career wishing you'd pursued the creative work you actually wanted. Safe but unfulfilling.
- **3D Animation risk**: You pursue your creative passion but graduate into a contracting industry with AI eating entry-level jobs. You spend your late 20s freelancing between contracts, earning less than your peers, wondering if the practical choice was better.
- **Health Sciences risk**: You start without a clear graduate school plan, finish a BSc, discover the next step is harder than expected, and find yourself in a weak position at 24 with a credential that doesn't open the doors you hoped.

Which of these failure modes frightens you more? That's information about what you value.

---

### Q32. Have you talked to people actually doing these jobs?

**Prompt:** All of this research is secondary. Primary sources matter most.

Before making a final decision, you should speak with:
- [ ] A practicing architect in an Ontario firm (ask them about Year 1 salary, day-to-day work, whether they'd do it again)
- [ ] A P.Eng. who went through AE or a related engineering path
- [ ] A working 3D animator or VFX artist (ask about job stability, crunch, contract gaps, and whether the Sheridan name still opens doors)
- [ ] A Health Sciences BSc graduate who did NOT go to medical school (what are they doing now?)
- [ ] A medical student or resident who started in Health Sciences (was it worth it?)
- [ ] Current students in each program (ask what surprised them)

Write the names of 3 people you could reach out to in the next 2 weeks.
