## 1. Program Overview

The **Bachelor of Science in Health Sciences (BHSc)** is offered through the **Faculty of Health** at the University of Waterloo. The program takes a "cell to society" approach — examining biological and social factors that shape human health across all scales, from molecular biology to health policy.

### Study Streams

| Stream | Duration | Structure |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| **Regular** | 4 years | Full-time study, September–April |
| **Co-op** | 5 years | Alternating study and work terms |

### Co-op Schedule (5-Year Track)

| Year | Fall | Winter | Spring |
|------|------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Study | Study | Off |
| 2 | Study | **Work** | Study |
| 3 | **Work** | Study | **Work** |
| 4 | Study | **Work** | **Work** |
| 5 | Study | Study | — |

Total co-op experience: up to **20 months** across 4–5 work terms.

### Specializations & Concentrations

- **Minors:** Addictions, Mental Health, and Policy; Gerontology
- **Options:** Aging Studies; Health Informatics
- **Specializations:** Health Research; Pre-Clinical (for professional school prep)

The program is **CEPH (Council on Education for Public Health) accredited**, which validates curriculum quality for public health career tracks.

## 3. Co-op Program — The Real Numbers

> **Key finding: Co-op rates for Health Sciences are significantly lower than the university-wide average and have experienced sharp recent deterioration.**

### University-Wide Co-op Employment Rates (Context)

| Term | University-Wide Rate |
|------|----------------------|
| Fall 2024 | **96.8%** |
| Spring 2024 | 93.2% |
| Spring 2020 (COVID) | 86.5% |
| **Spring 2025** | **75.0–77.3%** ⚠️ |

### Faculty of Health — Critical Data

| Term | Health Faculty Rate | Notes |
|------|--------------------|----|
| Fall 2024 | ~95%+ | Consistent with smaller faculties |
| **Spring 2025** | **71%** | **Lowest of all faculties** |

The spring 2025 term is the worst on record for the Health faculty — worse than COVID-era performance (86.5% in Spring 2020). By May 21, 2025, approximately **2,149 students across all faculties** remained unemployed, with Health Sciences the hardest hit.

**Why?** The Health Sciences job market, unlike Engineering or CS, is heavily tied to:
- **Public sector hiring cycles** (government health agencies)
- **Hospital and clinic hiring freezes** amid Ontario healthcare funding pressures
- **Macroeconomic conditions** — Statistics Canada data showed elevated unemployment among 15–24 year-olds in the same period

> **Realistic expectation:** In good years, Health co-op employment tracks 90–95%. In economic downturns or tight public-sector hiring cycles, it can drop to the low 70s. Do **not** assume the 96.8% university headline applies to Health Sciences students.

### Comparison to Other Faculties

| Faculty | Spring 2025 Rate |
|---------|-----------------|
| Engineering | 78.8% (highest) |
| University average | 75–77.3% |
| **Health** | **71% (lowest)** |

### Estimated Total Co-op Income (Over 20 Months)

At average rates: ~$63,500–$68,000 gross over the 5-year program.

This compares unfavorably to:
- **Computer Science** co-op: often $25–$50+/hour average
- **Engineering** co-op: $22–$35+/hour average
- **Pharmacy** co-op: $22–$32/hour average

> **Note:** The minimum provincial wage in Ontario (2025) is $17.20/hour — Health Sciences first-term co-op wages barely clear this threshold. The program's co-op financial return is modest. The primary value is **experience and credential signaling**, not income.

## 7. Job Market Outlook (2025–2030)

### Canadian Healthcare Sector — Macro Conditions

| Indicator | Data |
|-----------|------|
| Healthcare workers in Canada | 1,339,900 (7.8% of workforce, 2024) |
| Vacancy rate (2024) | **5.0%** — highest of all sectors |
| Health vacancies as % of all vacancies | **15.0%** (86,540 positions in 2024) |
| Ontario healthcare growth 2024 | +2.7% (+71,200 jobs over 2022–2024) |
| Average healthcare wage (Ontario) | $34.57/hour vs. $30.50 provincial avg |
| Projected shortage — doctors by 2031 | **78,000** |
| Projected shortage — nurses by 2030 | **117,600** |

### Structural Demand Drivers

1. **Aging population:** By 2031, ~25% of Canadians will be 65+, sharply increasing healthcare consumption
2. **Persistent vacancies:** Health vacancy rate rose from 2.1% (2016) to 5.8% (2024) — a structural, not cyclical, problem
3. **Remote gap:** Rural/remote health vacancy rates 2–3x higher than urban; registered nurses face 13.7% remote vacancy rate
4. **Government investment:** Ontario planned 260 new undergraduate medical seats; Federal Primary Care Act announced

### Role-Specific Outlook (Canada, 2024–2033)

| Career | Outlook | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Registered Nurse | Strong demand, 7.0% vacancy rate | Requires nursing degree |
| Occupational Therapist | Strong | Government calls for more seats |
| Physiotherapist | Strong | Strong private market |
| Epidemiologist | **Balanced** (moderate in ON/QC) | 16,000 employed nationally; balanced supply/demand through 2033 |
| Public Health Analyst | Growing | Government and NGO expansion |
| Health Policy Analyst | Stable | Government budget-dependent |
| Clinical Research | Growing | Pharma and clinical trial growth |

### Headwinds to Watch

| Risk | Impact |
|------|--------|
| **Reduced immigration targets** | Canada cut permanent resident target from 500K → 395K (2025); slower population growth may dampen healthcare demand growth |
| **Public sector hiring freezes** | Ontario health spending constraints directly affect co-op and early-career placements |
| **AI in healthcare** | Administrative and diagnostic support roles face automation; clinical and research roles more resilient (see Anthropic Economic Index data below) |
| **Degree saturation** | More health sciences graduates chasing the same early-career roles; competition intensifying |

### Anthropic Economic Index Data: AI in Healthcare & Life Sciences (January 2026)

The **Anthropic Economic Index v4** (January 2026) provides real-world AI usage data from Claude that directly measures how healthcare and life science professionals interact with AI.

#### How Healthcare Professionals Use AI

Healthcare Practitioners and Technical workers have a **unique** AI interaction profile — they use AI primarily for **learning**, not production:

| Mode | Healthcare Practitioners | All Occupations Average |
|------|------------------------|------------------------|
| **Learning** (information-seeking) | **53%** | ~15–25% |
| **Directive** (automation) | **28%** | ~35–45% |
| **Task Iteration** (augmentation) | **19%** | ~30–40% |

**Key insight**: Healthcare has the **highest learning share of any occupational group** — more than double the next-highest group (Life/Physical/Social Science at 35%). This means health professionals predominantly use AI to understand and research, not to automate clinical work. This is consistent with the profession's emphasis on evidence-based practice, continuing education, and regulatory caution around automated clinical decisions.

**Top 5 occupational groups by learning share:**
1. Healthcare Practitioners & Technical: **53%**
2. Life, Physical & Social Science: **35%**
3. Architecture & Engineering: **23%**
4. Business & Financial Operations: **28%**
5. Sales & Related: **27%**

#### Effective AI Coverage: Task Coverage vs. Real-World Impact

The report distinguishes between how many tasks AI *can* do vs. how much of a worker's *actual day* it can handle:

| Occupation | Task Coverage | Effective Coverage | Why the Difference |
|------------|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Medical Transcriptionists** | ~40% | **~51%** | AI covers the most time-intensive, frequent tasks |
| **Radiologists** | Lower | **~35%** | AI excels at core knowledge work (image interpretation, reports) |
| **Microbiologists** | ~50% | **~23%** | AI cannot perform hands-on lab research with specialized equipment |

**For Health Sciences graduates**: The pattern is clear — AI handles knowledge-intensive analytical tasks well but cannot replace hands-on clinical, laboratory, or in-person care work. Roles that combine both (e.g., clinical research, diagnostics) see mixed impact; roles that are primarily physical/clinical (nursing, physiotherapy, occupational therapy) are well-protected.

#### The Complement Effect in Healthcare

Using the **elasticity of substitution (σ)** framework:
- **σ < 1 (complements)**: When AI handles diagnostic data analysis, the clinician's judgment in interpreting results and making treatment decisions becomes *more* economically valuable
- Healthcare's **bottleneck tasks** (hands-on care, lab work, in-person patient interaction) cannot be automated, and they constrain how much AI can increase total productivity
- When tasks are complements, aggregate productivity growth drops from **1.8 to 0.7–0.9 percentage points** annually — healthcare is a prime example of this constraint
- However, this same bottleneck effect **increases the economic value of human clinical skills** — the tasks AI cannot do become the most valuable parts of healthcare work

#### Deskilling vs. Upskilling in Health

- AI tends to automate the **most skill-intensive** analytical tasks (e.g., radiological interpretation at ~18.7 years predicted education) while leaving humans with physical/administrative tasks
- For **diagnostic-heavy roles** (radiology, pathology): risk of deskilling as AI handles core analytical work
- For **clinical-contact roles** (nursing, physiotherapy, occupational therapy): **upskilling** — AI handles paperwork and analysis, freeing clinicians to focus on complex patient care and professional judgment
- For **Health Sciences BSc graduates** specifically: AI strengthens the case for pursuing **professional credentials** (MD, RN, OT, PT) that involve irreplaceable hands-on work, rather than stopping at analyst/coordinator roles that are more automatable

#### Canada-Specific Context

- Canada ranks in the **highest tier** of the Anthropic AI Usage Index, alongside the US, UK, and Japan
- Healthcare usage on Claude.ai is part of a "long tail" of human-facing categories where users primarily seek advice and information
- Higher-income countries (including Canada) use AI more for personal health advice and professional work, while lower-income countries use it more for coursework

### Verdict on Job Market

**Health as a sector is structurally undersupplied and growing.** However, a BSc Health Sciences is not a direct ticket to in-demand roles — nurses, doctors, OTs, and PTs require specialized professional programs beyond the BSc. The direct-employment market for BSc-only graduates is **competitive for mid-tier analyst and coordinator roles** ($45,000–$65,000 entry range). The strong job market benefits those who pursue further credentials most.

## 8. Should You Study This Major? — Decision Framework

### Choose Health Sciences at UWaterloo IF:

- **You have a clear professional school goal** (medicine, pharmacy, OT, PT, public health) and want a rigorous pre-professional foundation
- **You want flexibility** — the curriculum satisfies prerequisites for most Canadian medical schools and many professional programs
- **The co-op experience matters to you** even at lower wages — clinical research, health agency, and hospital placements build resume credibility beyond what most universities offer
- **You're interested in public health, epidemiology, or health policy** as a career and plan to do graduate school
- **You score in the high 80s+** and want a respected research-intensive Canadian university on your transcript

### Be Cautious IF:

- **You expect a high-paying career directly from the BSc** — direct employment salaries are $45,000–$65,000 at entry, and many graduates spend additional years in professional school before reaching high income
- **Financial return on co-op is a priority** — Health co-op pays the least among UWaterloo programs (~$18–21/hour average); if you need income during studies, consider Engineering or CS
- **Job security during co-op is critical to you** — the Spring 2025 collapse to 71% employment was alarming; economic downturns hit the Health faculty hardest
- **You have no plan beyond the BSc** — without a clear next step (grad school, professional program), a Health Sciences degree alone has limited differentiation from a Biology or Life Sciences degree at other universities

## 9. Bottom Line

**The UWaterloo Health Sciences degree is a strong pre-professional foundation, not a standalone career ticket.**

The most important insight from this research:

1. **Co-op rates tell a split story.** In stable years: 90–95%. In Spring 2025: 71% — the lowest in the university and worse than COVID. Budget for volatility.

2. **The 95% employed/in-school stat is real but requires context.** Nearly half of those graduates are in further school. Direct BSc employment is ~47%, paying $45,000–$65,000 to start.

3. **The healthcare sector is structurally booming** — 86,540 open positions, a 5% vacancy rate (highest of all sectors), and aging-population demand that won't abate. But the high-demand roles (nursing, medicine, OT, PT) require credentials beyond this BSc.

4. **Study this program if you have a plan.** The degree is excellent preparation for professional or graduate school. Without that plan, the financial return is below average for a 5-year UWaterloo degree.

5. **Prediction for 2025–2030:** Health sector demand will remain strong, but **early-career analyst and coordinator roles will become more competitive** as more students graduate from health programs. Roles requiring professional licensure (RN, OT, PT, MD) will remain in shortage. AI will automate administrative and diagnostic-analytical tasks; clinical, research, and policy roles will grow. The Anthropic Economic Index confirms healthcare practitioners use AI primarily for **learning (53%)**, not automation — and hands-on clinical roles are structurally protected by bottleneck tasks AI cannot perform.
