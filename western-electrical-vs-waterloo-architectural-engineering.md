### 4.3 What this means for your résumé at graduation

Western and Waterloo produce graduates with very different work-experience profiles, and the difference is structural, not effort-driven.

A Waterloo ArchE student graduates with **~20 months of paid, progressive, AEC-industry co-op already completed and documented**, plus ~$85,000–$95,000 of pre-tax earnings deposited along the way (Engineering co-op averages run Term 1 $21.67/hr → Term 5 $29.60/hr, [uwaterloo.ca](https://uwaterloo.ca/hire/how-to-hire/co-op-student-earnings)). Twelve of those months can be counted toward the PEO 48-month experience requirement on the way to P.Eng. ([peo.on.ca](https://www.peo.on.ca/apply/become-professional-engineer)). This is locked in by the program structure — there is no version of the degree without it.

A Western EE student graduates with anywhere from **0 to ~16 months** of internship experience depending on whether they opted in to the Internship Program or the "with Co-op" track ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/tc/undergraduate/integrated-engineering/index.html); [eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/careers/students/undergraduate-co-op/index.html)). A motivated EE student who completes the 16-month internship lands close to a Waterloo ArchE student's experience profile, with a single annualized salary near $50,000 ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/careers/students/index.html)). A student who does not opt in graduates with no professional work experience on their résumé.

The honest framing: at Waterloo, ~20 months is the floor; at Western, ~16 months is the ceiling. Both directions are real — students who would not naturally seek out a co-op program may prefer Western's optional model; students who want the financial offset and résumé guarantee built in may prefer Waterloo's mandatory one.

### 5.2 Specialization streams

**Western EE — three Year-4 specialization streams** ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/undergraduate/programs/electrical.html)):

1. **Information Communication and Transmission** — wireless systems, communications design, antennas, digital signal processing.
2. **Power Systems** — sustainable energy sources, conventional power generation and distribution.
3. **Biomedical Engineering** — signal processing, imaging, medical device design (concurrent-degree option with the School of Biomedical Engineering).

Concurrent / dual-degree options confirmed on the same page: **Electrical Engineering and Business** (Ivey HBA) and **Electrical Engineering and Law**. The progression-sheets index also lists an **Electrical + AI Systems Engineering "Option M"** track ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/electrical/undergraduate/Current-Students/Program-Progression-Sheets.html)).

**Waterloo ArchE — two Year-4/5 specialization tracks** (in repo: `waterloo-architectural-engineering.md`, citing uwaterloo.ca):

1. **Building Structures** — structural analysis and design for buildings.
2. **Building Systems** — HVAC, electrical, plumbing, lighting integration; energy modeling; envelope.

Diploma emphases can be added in AI, Computing, Environmental Engineering, Management Sciences, Entrepreneurship, Software Engineering, Mechatronics, or Sustainability.

### 5.3 Where the curricula overlap and diverge

Both programs share an engineering-core spine in Years 1–2 — calculus, linear algebra, differential equations, statics/dynamics, an introductory electrical-circuits sequence, and a programming course. Both demand a capstone in the final year. Both teach Revit/CAD-class building modeling tools (one as a passing exposure, the other as core craft).

After Year 2 the programs diverge sharply:

- **In both:** electrical circuits, basic electromagnetics, control concepts, and ethics/law (Western: ELI 4110G; Waterloo: through the engineering core).
- **Only in Western EE:** signal processing, communications theory, electromagnetic theory, microprocessors, VLSI, image processing, and a deep technical-elective bench that lets a student concentrate in tech-industry-adjacent specializations (AI hardware, photonics, embedded, networking) ([progression sheet PDF](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/files/undergraduate/2023-Progression-Sheets/ECE_OPA_Year_1_in_2020-or-later-FW2025.pdf)).
- **Only in Waterloo ArchE:** structural mechanics for buildings, HVAC and building-systems integration, building envelope, energy and sustainability modeling, **Design Studio every single term**, and a Year 3 immersion at the Cambridge architecture school working alongside MArch students (in repo: `waterloo-architectural-engineering.md`).

The simplest summary: a Western EE graduate has been trained to engineer **electrical signals and energy** at any scale from chip to power grid; a Waterloo ArchE graduate has been trained to engineer **buildings end-to-end** from structure through envelope through systems. The former is broad and industry-agnostic; the latter is narrow and industry-specific.

## 6. Professional Licensing Pathways

Both programs lead to the **same licence — P.Eng. through Professional Engineers Ontario (PEO)** — under the same statutory rules, because both are CEAB-accredited engineering degrees ([engineerscanada.ca](https://engineerscanada.ca/accreditation/accredited-programs/institution?page=7); [peo.on.ca](https://www.peo.on.ca/apply/become-professional-engineer)).

### 6.1 The shared P.Eng. pathway

```
CEAB-accredited engineering degree (Western EE BESc — 4 yrs, OR Waterloo ArchE BASc — 5 yrs)
    ↓
Apply to PEO; register as Engineering Intern (EIT)
    ↓
National Professional Practice Exam (NPPE) — 3-hour closed-book on ethics, law, and practice
    ↓
48 months acceptable engineering experience under a P.Eng., ≥ 12 months in a Canadian jurisdiction
    (up to 12 months of qualifying co-op / internship completed after Year 2 may count)
    ↓
PEO competency-based assessment + good-character review
    ↓
P.Eng. licence — title-protected "Professional Engineer"
```

Source: [peo.on.ca — Become a Professional Engineer](https://www.peo.on.ca/apply/become-professional-engineer); Western co-op page confirming the 12-month PEO credit clause ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/careers/students/undergraduate-co-op/index.html)).

| **Co-op credit toward 48-month PEO requirement** | Up to **12 months** if the student opts into the Internship / "with Co-op" track ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/careers/students/undergraduate-co-op/index.html)) | Up to **12 months** of post-Year-2 co-op ([peo.on.ca](https://www.peo.on.ca/apply/become-professional-engineer)) |
| **Typical post-grad time to P.Eng.** | ~3 years if the student took the optional internship; ~4 years if they did not | ~3 years (co-op credit is essentially automatic) |
| **Total time HS → P.Eng.** | ~7–8 years (4-yr degree + ~3–4 yrs experience) | ~8 years (5-yr degree + ~3 yrs experience) |
| **Protected title** | "P.Eng." / "Professional Engineer" ([peo.on.ca](https://www.peo.on.ca/apply/become-professional-engineer)) | Same ([peo.on.ca](https://www.peo.on.ca/apply/become-professional-engineer)) |

### 6.3 Common adjacent credentials

Beyond P.Eng., graduates in each discipline tend to layer on different industry credentials:

- **Western EE graduates** commonly add credentials such as **PMP** (Project Management Professional, for engineering-management tracks) and **IEEE-member-only specialty certifications** (e.g., IEEE Power & Energy, IEEE Communications) where they specialize.
- **Waterloo ArchE graduates** commonly add **LEED AP** (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design Accredited Professional, [cagbc.org](https://www.cagbc.org/news-resources/cagbc-news/greening-canadas-buildings/)) and **WELL AP** credentials, which are increasingly required for sustainability-track building consulting roles.

These adjacent credentials are not licensing-track — they are market-signaling credentials a graduate adds during the first 5–10 years of practice. A Waterloo ArchE graduate stamping engineering drawings still does so as a P.Eng., not as a LEED AP; a Western EE graduate leading a project still does so as a P.Eng., not as a PMP. Both programs converge on the same statutory licence; they diverge on which non-statutory credentials the market rewards alongside it.
