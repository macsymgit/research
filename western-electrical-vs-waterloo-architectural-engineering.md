### 4.3 What this means for your résumé at graduation

Western and Waterloo produce graduates with very different work-experience profiles, and the difference is structural, not effort-driven.

A Waterloo ArchE student graduates with **~20 months of paid, progressive, AEC-industry co-op already completed and documented**, plus ~$85,000–$95,000 of pre-tax earnings deposited along the way (Engineering co-op averages run Term 1 $21.67/hr → Term 5 $29.60/hr, [uwaterloo.ca](https://uwaterloo.ca/hire/how-to-hire/co-op-student-earnings)). Twelve of those months can be counted toward the PEO 48-month experience requirement on the way to P.Eng. ([peo.on.ca](https://www.peo.on.ca/apply/become-professional-engineer)). This is locked in by the program structure — there is no version of the degree without it.

A Western EE student graduates with anywhere from **0 to ~16 months** of internship experience depending on whether they opted in to the Internship Program or the "with Co-op" track ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/tc/undergraduate/integrated-engineering/index.html); [eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/careers/students/undergraduate-co-op/index.html)). A motivated EE student who completes the 16-month internship lands close to a Waterloo ArchE student's experience profile, with a single annualized salary near $50,000 ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/careers/students/index.html)). A student who does not opt in graduates with no professional work experience on their résumé.

The honest framing: at Waterloo, ~20 months is the floor; at Western, ~16 months is the ceiling. Both directions are real — students who would not naturally seek out a co-op program may prefer Western's optional model; students who want the financial offset and résumé guarantee built in may prefer Waterloo's mandatory one.

### 5.2 Specialization streams

**Western EE — three Year-4 specialization streams** ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/undergraduate/programs/electrical.html)):

1. **Information Communication and Transmission** — wireless systems, communications design, antennas, digital signal processing.
2. **Power Systems** — sustainable energy sources, conventional power generation and distribution.
3. **Biomedical Engineering** — signal processing, imaging, medical device design (note: Biomedical Engineering can be pursued as a Year-4 EE specialization stream **or** as a separate concurrent-degree option with the School of Biomedical Engineering, per Western's program page).

Concurrent / dual-degree options confirmed on the same page: **Electrical Engineering and Business** (Ivey HBA) and **Electrical Engineering and Law**. The progression-sheets index also lists an **Electrical + AI Systems Engineering "Option M"** track ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/electrical/undergraduate/Current-Students/Program-Progression-Sheets.html)).

**Waterloo ArchE — two Year-4/5 specialization tracks** (in repo: `waterloo-architectural-engineering.md`, citing uwaterloo.ca):

1. **Building Structures** — structural analysis and design for buildings.
2. **Building Systems** — HVAC, electrical, plumbing, lighting integration; energy modeling; envelope.

Diploma emphases can be added in AI, Computing, Environmental Engineering, Management Sciences, Entrepreneurship, Software Engineering, Mechatronics, or Sustainability.

### 5.3 Where the curricula overlap and diverge

Both programs share an engineering-core spine in Years 1–2 — calculus, linear algebra, differential equations, statics/dynamics, an introductory electrical-circuits sequence, and a programming course. Both demand a capstone in the final year. Both teach Revit/CAD-class building modeling tools (one as a passing exposure, the other as core craft).

After Year 2 the programs diverge sharply:

- **In both:** electrical circuits, basic electromagnetics, control concepts, and ethics/law (Western: ELI 4110G; Waterloo: through the engineering core).
- **Only in Western EE:** signal processing, communications theory, electromagnetic theory, microprocessors, VLSI, image processing, and a deep technical-elective bench that lets a student concentrate in tech-industry-adjacent specializations (AI hardware, photonics, embedded, networking) ([progression sheet PDF](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/files/undergraduate/2023-Progression-Sheets/ECE_OPA_Year_1_in_2020-or-later-FW2025.pdf)).
- **Only in Waterloo ArchE:** structural mechanics for buildings, HVAC and building-systems integration, building envelope, energy and sustainability modeling, **Design Studio every single term**, and a Year 3 immersion at the Cambridge architecture school working alongside MArch students (in repo: `waterloo-architectural-engineering.md`).

The simplest summary: a Western EE graduate has been trained to engineer **electrical signals and energy** at any scale from chip to power grid; a Waterloo ArchE graduate has been trained to engineer **buildings end-to-end** from structure through envelope through systems. The former is broad and industry-agnostic; the latter is narrow and industry-specific.

## 6. Professional Licensing Pathways

Both programs lead to the **same licence — P.Eng. through Professional Engineers Ontario (PEO)** — under the same statutory rules, because both are CEAB-accredited engineering degrees ([engineerscanada.ca](https://engineerscanada.ca/accreditation/accredited-programs/institution?page=7); [peo.on.ca](https://www.peo.on.ca/apply/become-professional-engineer)).

### 6.1 The shared P.Eng. pathway

```
CEAB-accredited engineering degree (Western EE BESc — 4 yrs, OR Waterloo ArchE BASc — 5 yrs)
    ↓
Apply to PEO; register as Engineering Intern (EIT)
    ↓
National Professional Practice Exam (NPPE) — 3-hour closed-book on ethics, law, and practice
    ↓
48 months acceptable engineering experience under a P.Eng., ≥ 12 months in a Canadian jurisdiction
    (up to 12 months of qualifying co-op / internship completed after Year 2 may count)
    ↓
PEO competency-based assessment + good-character review
    ↓
P.Eng. licence — title-protected "Professional Engineer"
```

Source: [peo.on.ca — Become a Professional Engineer](https://www.peo.on.ca/apply/become-professional-engineer); Western co-op page confirming the 12-month PEO credit clause ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/careers/students/undergraduate-co-op/index.html)).

### 6.2 Side-by-side licensing comparison

### 6.3 Common adjacent credentials

Beyond P.Eng., graduates in each discipline tend to layer on different industry credentials:

- **Western EE graduates** commonly add credentials such as **PMP** (Project Management Professional, for engineering-management tracks) and **IEEE-member-only specialty certifications** (e.g., IEEE Power & Energy, IEEE Communications) where they specialize.
- **Waterloo ArchE graduates** commonly add **LEED AP** (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design Accredited Professional, [cagbc.org](https://www.cagbc.org/news-resources/cagbc-news/greening-canadas-buildings/)) and **WELL AP** credentials, which are increasingly required for sustainability-track building consulting roles.

These adjacent credentials are not licensing-track — they are market-signaling credentials a graduate adds during the first 5–10 years of practice. A Waterloo ArchE graduate stamping engineering drawings still does so as a P.Eng., not as a LEED AP; a Western EE graduate leading a project still does so as a P.Eng., not as a PMP. Both programs converge on the same statutory licence; they diverge on which non-statutory credentials the market rewards alongside it.

### 7.3 Reading the salary picture honestly

For the median Ontario engineer, EE and ArchE land within a few thousand dollars of one another. The visible asymmetry is in the upper tail: a Western EE graduate who lands in tech-corridor semiconductors or AI hardware can clear NOC 21310's 90th percentile of ~$171K/yr ([Job Bank](https://www.jobbank.gc.ca/marketreport/wages-occupation/17815/ON)) and, with equity, exceed it; a Waterloo ArchE graduate who reaches senior P.Eng. status at a major building consultancy clusters around the NOC 21300 90th percentile of ~$150K/yr ([Job Bank](https://www.jobbank.gc.ca/marketreport/wages-occupation/22376/ON)) plus a typically smaller bonus pool.

Two methodology hedges before any reader treats these as forecasts. First, NOC 21300 is a **proxy** for ArchE and is dominated by structural / civil work — building-systems-only ArchE careers may diverge upward (mechanical/electrical specialty work) or downward (BIM-coordinator-track) from this aggregate. Second, NOC 21310 aggregates an extremely broad set of electrical/electronics sub-roles; an EE graduate's actual wage trajectory depends heavily on whether they enter tech, utilities, or consulting, and the spread within NOC 21310 is wider than the spread within NOC 21300. Both directions of risk are real.

### 10.2 Net cost after co-op / internship earnings

The single largest financial difference between the two programs is on the earnings side, not the cost side. Because Waterloo's co-op is mandatory and 5 terms long, every ArchE graduate receives the offset; because Western's internship is optional, only opt-in students see a Western-side offset.

**Western EE — optional Internship Program offset:**

A student who completes the 12–16 month "Year-Long Co-op" cited by the Engineering Career Centre at an average annual salary of $50,000 ([eng.uwo.ca](https://www.eng.uwo.ca/careers/students/index.html)) earns approximately **$50,000–$67,000 pre-tax** over the internship window, depending on length. A student who does not opt in earns $0 from the program. The Western net cost therefore spans a wide band:

- **No-internship student:** ~$120,000–$145,000 net program cost (4 years tuition + ancillaries + housing + food, no work-experience offset).
- **Internship-completing student:** ~$70,000–$95,000 net program cost (same gross minus ~$50K–$67K internship earnings); this also adds ~6–12 months of calendar time to graduation.

**Waterloo ArchE — mandatory co-op offset (worked example):**

Per the live UW co-op-earnings page, Engineering co-op averages ran Term 1 $21.67/hr → Term 5 $29.60/hr for the most recent published cohort ([uwaterloo.ca](https://uwaterloo.ca/hire/how-to-hire/co-op-student-earnings)). Worked example with explicit assumptions:

- Hours per term: 16 weeks × 40 hrs/wk = **640 hrs** (lower bound) to 16 weeks × 45 hrs/wk = **720 hrs** (upper bound).
- 5 terms × weighted average ~$25.59/hr = **~$25.59/hr × ~3,200–3,600 hrs ≈ ~$82,000–$92,000 pre-tax (~$81,888 at the 640 hr/term floor; ~$92,124 at the 720 hr/term ceiling)** total over the degree.
- Term-by-term illustrative: $21.67 × 640 = ~$13,869 → $23.55 × 640 = ~$15,072 → $25.67 × 640 = ~$16,429 → $27.46 × 640 = ~$17,574 → $29.60 × 640 = ~$18,944 → **~$81,888** at the 640-hour-per-term floor; the same calculation at 720 hrs/term yields ~$92,124.
- Net program cost (5-year tuition + ancillaries + housing + food, ~$155K–$180K, **minus** ~$82K–$92K co-op earnings): **~$65,000–$95,000**.

### 10.3 Net cost summary

| Scenario | Gross 4–5 yr cost | Work-experience earnings | **Net cost** |
|----------|-------------------|--------------------------|--------------|
| **Western EE, no internship** | ~$120K–$145K (4 yrs) | $0 | **~$120K–$145K** |
| **Western EE, 16-month internship** | ~$120K–$145K + ~6–12 mo time-to-grad | ~$50K–$67K | **~$70K–$95K** |
| **Waterloo ArchE, mandatory co-op** | ~$155K–$180K (5 yrs) | ~$82K–$92K | **~$65K–$95K** |

The honest summary: when a Western EE student takes the optional internship, the net-cost ranges of the two programs converge — Waterloo costs more in tuition because it is a year longer, but the mandatory co-op earnings offset closes most of that gap. When a Western EE student does not take the internship, Western is meaningfully cheaper net-of-earnings, but the student also graduates with no work experience on their résumé. Both directions are real and depend entirely on whether the Western student opts in to the internship track.

## 11. Decision Framework: Which Is Right for You?

The questions below are designed to surface the structural trade-offs of the two programs. Each question maps directly to one of the asymmetries documented in §1–§10. There is no "right" answer — the right program depends on which trade-offs you can live with.

### 11.1 The 12 questions

**1. Do you want the broadest possible engineering toolkit, or a focused building-industry specialization?**
- *Broad toolkit (signals, power, comms, embedded, AI hardware)* → **Western EE**
- *Focused building specialization (structures + systems + envelope + sustainability)* → **Waterloo ArchE**
- *Rationale:* EE is one of the broadest engineering disciplines; ArchE is one of the most narrowly scoped. Breadth gives industry-mobility optionality; specialization gives deeper craft inside one industry.

**2. Is mandatory paid work experience a hard requirement?**
- *Yes — I want guaranteed co-op income and résumé experience baked in* → **Waterloo ArchE** (~20 months mandatory)
- *No — I prefer flexibility to opt in or out* → **Western EE** (12–16 month internship is optional)
- *Rationale:* Waterloo locks in ~20 months of co-op and ~$82K–$92K of pre-tax earnings ([uwaterloo.ca co-op earnings](https://uwaterloo.ca/hire/how-to-hire/co-op-student-earnings)). Western's internship requires opting in.

**3. What's your tolerance for a 5-year degree vs. a 4-year degree?**
- *I want to be in the workforce sooner* → **Western EE** (4 years, or ~5 with optional internship)
- *I'm comfortable with 5 years if it includes structured co-op* → **Waterloo ArchE**
- *Rationale:* The year of difference is real even before the optional Western internship is factored in.

**4. Are you drawn to design culture or to broad engineering science?**
- *Design culture (studio every term, Cambridge year alongside MArch students)* → **Waterloo ArchE**
- *Engineering science (signals, electromagnetics, control systems, devices)* → **Western EE**
- *Rationale:* ArchE is the only one of the two with a design studio embedded every term.

**5. How important is salary ceiling vs. salary floor?**
- *Ceiling matters more — I want exposure to tech-sector total compensation including equity* → **Western EE**
- *Floor matters more — I want a predictable, regulated, building-industry trajectory* → **Waterloo ArchE**
- *Rationale:* NOC 21310 90th-percentile $82.42/hr (~$171K/yr) vs. NOC 21300 90th-percentile $72.12/hr (~$150K/yr) ([Job Bank 21310](https://www.jobbank.gc.ca/marketreport/wages-occupation/17815/ON); [Job Bank 21300](https://www.jobbank.gc.ca/marketreport/wages-occupation/22376/ON)).

**6. How much weight do you put on the 2025–2027 Ontario job-market outlook?**
- *A lot — I want the higher-rated outlook for the immediate post-grad window* → **Waterloo ArchE** (NOC 21300 Ontario "Moderate")
- *Less — I'm betting on tech-corridor sub-segments outperforming the aggregate* → **Western EE** (NOC 21310 Ontario "Limited" aggregate, but tech sub-segments stronger)
- *Rationale:* The official Statistics-Canada-derived ratings differ ([Job Bank 21310 outlook](https://www.jobbank.gc.ca/marketreport/outlook-occupation/17815/ON); [Job Bank 21300 outlook](https://www.jobbank.gc.ca/marketreport/outlook-occupation/22376/ON)).

**7. Do you prefer a smaller-city campus feel or a tech-corridor employer pool?**
- *Smaller-city campus feel, single concentrated campus* → **Western EE / London**
- *Tech-corridor employer pool, two-campus mobility* → **Waterloo ArchE / Waterloo + Cambridge**
- *Rationale:* London (~440K metro) vs. KW (~575K metro, denser tech employer cluster).

**8. How comfortable are you with construction-cycle exposure?**
- *Uncomfortable — I want recession-resilience across many industries* → **Western EE**
- *Comfortable — I see the net-zero / infrastructure cycle as a long tailwind* → **Waterloo ArchE**
- *Rationale:* ArchE careers correlate to construction-investment cycles; EE careers diversify across utilities, tech, telecom, defence.

**9. Is the regulatory floor against AI displacement important to you?**
- *Yes, and I want it tied to a P.Eng. seal that touches physical infrastructure people inhabit* → **Waterloo ArchE** (P.Eng. seals on building drawings are statutorily required)
- *Yes, and I want it tied to a broader P.Eng. that covers many industries* → **Western EE** (same statutory licence under PEO)
- *Rationale:* Both routes lead to P.Eng. via PEO ([peo.on.ca](https://www.peo.on.ca/apply/become-professional-engineer)); the regulatory floor is the same statutory licence.

**10. How much do you want a Year 3 immersion at an architecture school?**
- *A lot — I want to live and work in a design-school environment for a full academic year* → **Waterloo ArchE**
- *Not at all — I want a steady engineering campus through all four years* → **Western EE**
- *Rationale:* This is a structural feature of ArchE, not an option.

**11. How much weight do you put on co-op-driven net cost?**
- *A lot — I want mandatory co-op earnings to offset most of program cost* → **Waterloo ArchE** (~$65K–$95K net cost after co-op)
- *Less — I prefer the lower 4-year tuition footprint and accept the wider net-cost band* → **Western EE** (~$70K–$145K net cost depending on internship participation)
- *Rationale:* See §10.3.

**12. Where do you imagine yourself in 10 years?**
- *Senior hardware/firmware/power/communications engineer at a tech, utility, telecom, or defence company* → **Western EE**
- *Senior building-systems / structural / sustainability P.Eng. at a building consultancy or developer* → **Waterloo ArchE**
- *Rationale:* The two pathways converge on P.Eng. but produce graduates aimed at structurally different industries.

### 11.2 If you're still torn

If after working through all twelve questions the decision still feels even, the practical tiebreakers are: **your relationship with mandatory co-op** (Waterloo's 5-term rotation is structural — you will be alternating school and work every 4 months for five years; this is a lifestyle, not a feature), **your draw to design studio culture** (ArchE has it every term, EE does not), and **your geographic comfort** (London single-campus vs. Waterloo + Cambridge two-campus). Salary is essentially a wash at the median; the high-end ceiling tilts EE, the regulated demand floor tilts ArchE. Both are CEAB-accredited, both lead to the same P.Eng., both employ their graduates well. The decision is genuinely about which trade-offs you want to live with.
